Safeguarding Healthcare Data in the Digital Era

United Healthcare - Data Breach - CIA Triad

In a recent cybersecurity incident, United Healthcare, one of the nation's largest healthcare providers, fell victim to a sophisticated cyberattack, sending shockwaves through the healthcare industry. The attack, now linked to the ransomware group Blackcat (aka. ALPHV or Noberus), targeted sensitive databases of Change Healthcare, United Health's subsidiary. The exploits potentially exposed "more than 6 TB of highly selective data" belonging to millions of patients through unauthorized access. The breach not only jeopardizes patient privacy, but also raises concerns about the CIA triad as the foundation for HIPAA compliance within healthcare organizations in the digital age.


The CIA triad - confidentiality, integrity, and availability - forms the bedrock of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA). This latest attacks proves more resources and better strategies are needed for healthcare systems facing an increasing amount of cybersecurity challenges. Blackcat exploited vulnerabilities in United Healthcare's network infrastructure, highlighting the urgent need for stronger cybersecurity measures within the healthcare sector. The incident serves as a reminder of the growing threat landscape faced by providers, who are prime targets for cybercriminals due to the valuable data they possess. As the healthcare industry increasingly relies on digital systems to store and manage patient information, the assurance of the CIA triad for this data becomes paramount.


The repercussions of the United Healthcare cyberattack extend beyond the organization itself, forcing public trust in the security of healthcare systems nationwide to become questionable. Patients are left with concerns about the safety of their personal health information, while regulators and policymakers face pressure to enact stricter cybersecurity regulations that will safeguard patient data. Moving forward, healthcare organizations must invest in advanced threat detection and response capabilities, employee training, and proactive security measures to mitigate the risk of future cyberattacks and protect patient privacy.


The United Healthcare cyberattack emphasizes the critical importance of cybersecurity in the healthcare sector and the need for a coordinated effort to strengthen defenses against ever-evolving cyber threats. As technology continues to revolutionize healthcare delivery, organizations must prioritize cybersecurity as a vital component of their operations to uphold patient trust and ensure the confidentiality and security of sensitive medical information.

The Ransom Dilemma: To Pay or Not to Pay?

Ransomware - Extortion - Recovery - GRC Framework

Ransomware is a lucrative, yet sustainable, business model for cybercriminals. It puts every organization that uses technology at risk, might be easier to pay the ransom than recover backups, and supporting the attacker's business model will only lead to more ransomware. So, in today's digital landscape, organizations face a daunting decision when struck by ransomware: To pay or not to pay?


In 2021, Colonial Pipeline Company, owner of the largest refined oil pipeline system in the U.S., paid hackers $4.4 million in ransom for a decryption tool that restored oil operations, despite FBI and Department of Homeland Security recommendations that companies avoid paying ransoms. The CEO later testified before the U.S. Congress that the debilitating impact to the country's fuel supply drove the decision, but it remains a controversial solution.


What would your organization do if it was hit by a ransomware attack? This dilemma is not just about retrieving encrypted data but also navigating ethical, legal, and operational considerations. Within the Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) framework, this decision becomes complex.


Paying the ransom may seem like a quick fix to regain access to critical data and systems. However, it sets a dangerous precedent incentivizing cybercriminals and funding further criminal activity. From a governance perspective, this raises questions about ethical behavior.


As for risk, paying the ransom does not guarantee data recovery. On average, only 65% of the data is recovered, and only 8% of organizations manage to recover all data. In addition, these organizations may still face reputational damage, financial loss, and legal consequences.


From a compliance standpoint, organizations must align their decisions with industry standards and regulatory requirements. General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), for example, comes into play as a governing law on how personal data is used, stored, and processed. Through GDPR, if an organization is subject to ransomware, they must disclose data breaches, take appropriate action, and ensure proper security measures are in place. A suitable compliance regulation, but public knowledge of such instances can be further damaging to a company's reputation, in addition to the financial and legal trouble stemming from ransomware.


Ultimately, organizations cannot 100% prevent ransomware attacks. The best thing is assume you will be hit, and have plans in place to enable a quick response. This includes strengthening backups and testing restores on all critical business. If backups work, the cost of recovery will be less than paying the ransom for an uncertain outcome. Treat ransomware as a business decision. If the problem is visible across the organization, there will be fewer surprises if you get hit.


A holistic approach integrating governance, risk management, and compliance principles is essential in navigating ransomware and safeguarding organizational interests. This will smooth all actions in the response, including a decision whether you should or should not pay.


Never pay.
